# שְׁאוֹל (Sheol, underworld)

Picture a vast, shadowy realm beneath the earth's surface—not a specific location but a state of being where the dead exist in a diminished form, cut off from the vibrancy of life and from active relationship with God. A place of dust, darkness, and silence that all humanity eventually faces.

## Root

שׁ-א-ל (sh-'-l) - To ask/inquire or to go down/descend

Closest English mapping: "underworld", indicating the realm of the dead beneath the earth.

The etymology is debated among scholars:
- Possibly from שָׁאַל (sha'al, "to ask/inquire") - the place that "asks for" or demands all living
- Alternatively from שָׁעַל (sha'al, "to go down") - the place of descent

## Related Concepts

קֶבֶר (qever) - Grave/Tomb

- Physical burial place rather than the realm of the dead
- Concrete location rather than state of being
- "Joseph was put in a **coffin** in Egypt" (Genesis 50:26)

בּוֹר (bor) - Pit

- Often used as a synonym for Sheol
- Emphasizes depth and imprisonment
- "Those who go down to the **pit** cannot hope for your faithfulness" (Isaiah 38:18)

אֲבַדּוֹן (Abaddon) - Destruction/Place of Destruction

- More explicitly negative than Sheol
- Emphasizes ruin and perdition
- "**Destruction** and Death say, 'We have heard a rumor of it with our ears'" (Job 28:22)

צַלְמָוֶת (tsalmaveth) - Shadow of Death

- Poetic description of death's domain
- Emphasizes darkness and terror
- "Though I walk through the valley of the **shadow of death**" (Psalm 23:4)

תַּחְתִּית (tachtit) - Depths/Lowest Parts

- Used to describe the deepest regions
- Often modified as "lowest Sheol"
- "For a fire is kindled by my anger, and it burns to the **depths** of Sheol" (Deuteronomy 32:22)

Unlike these words, שְׁאוֹל (Sheol) serves as the most comprehensive term for the realm of the dead, incorporating aspects of all these related concepts.

## Compound Forms

### שְׁאוֹל תַּחְתִּית (Sheol tachtit) - "Lowest Underworld"

- Intensified form emphasizing extremity
- Deuteronomy 32:22 "For a fire is kindled by my anger, and it burns to the depths of **the lowest underworld**"
- Psalm 86:13 "You have delivered my soul from the depths of **the lowest underworld**"

### שַׁעֲרֵי שְׁאוֹל (sha'arei Sheol) - "Gates of Underworld"

- Emphasizes Sheol as a realm with boundaries
- Isaiah 38:10 "I said, In the middle of my days I must depart; I am consigned to **the gates of the underworld** for the rest of my years"
- Psalm 9:13 "Be gracious to me, O LORD! See my affliction from those who hate me, O you who lift me up from **the gates of the underworld**"

### חֶבְלֵי שְׁאוֹל (chevlei Sheol) - "Cords of Underworld"

- Depicts Sheol as ensnaring or binding
- 2 Samuel 22:6 "**The cords of the underworld** entangled me; the snares of death confronted me"
- Psalm 18:5 "**The cords of the underworld** surrounded me; the snares of death confronted me"

### בִּטְנֵי שְׁאוֹל (bitnei Sheol) - "Belly of Underworld"

- Portrays Sheol as a consuming entity
- Jonah 2:2 "Out of **the belly of the underworld** I called for help, and you heard my voice"

## Greek/Septuagint Usage

In the Septuagint (LXX), שְׁאוֹל is consistently translated as:

ᾅδης (Hades) - The Greek underworld

- Used 61 times in the LXX for Sheol
- Carries similar connotations of the realm of the dead
- Less personified than in Greek mythology

In the New Testament, ᾅδης (Hades) appears 10 times:

Matthew 11:23 "And you, Capernaum, will you be exalted to heaven? You will be brought down to **Hades**."

Matthew 16:18 "On this rock I will build my church, and the gates of **Hades** shall not prevail against it."

Luke 16:23 "In **Hades**, being in torment, he lifted up his eyes and saw Abraham far off and Lazarus at his side."

Acts 2:27 (quoting Psalm 16:10) "For you will not abandon my soul to **Hades**, or let your Holy One see corruption."

Acts 2:31 "He foresaw and spoke about the resurrection of the Christ, that he was not abandoned to **Hades**, nor did his flesh see corruption."

Revelation 1:18 "I am the living one. I died, and behold I am alive forevermore, and I have the keys of Death and **Hades**."

Revelation 6:8 "And I looked, and behold, a pale horse! And its rider's name was Death, and **Hades** followed him."

Revelation 20:13-14 "And the sea gave up the dead who were in it, Death and **Hades** gave up the dead who were in them... Then Death and **Hades** were thrown into the lake of fire."

## Patterns

### Descent and Ascent Pattern

Sheol appears in a consistent pattern of descent (going down) and occasional ascent (being brought up):

**Descent Terminology**

- יָרַד (yarad) - "go down" to Sheol
  - Genesis 37:35 "I shall **go down** to my son mourning to the underworld"
  - Numbers 16:30 "They **go down** alive into the underworld"
  - Job 7:9 "He who **goes down** to the underworld does not come up"

- שָׁכַב (shakav) - "lie down" in Sheol
  - Isaiah 14:11 "Your pomp is **brought down** to the underworld"
  - Ezekiel 32:21 "They have come down, they **lie still**"

**Ascent Terminology**

- עָלָה (alah) - "come up" from Sheol
  - 1 Samuel 2:6 "He brings down to the underworld and **raises up**"
  - Psalm 30:3 "O LORD, you have brought up my soul from the underworld"

This pattern emphasizes Sheol's location as "down" and salvation as "up," creating a spatial metaphor for spiritual states.

### Universal Destination Pattern

Sheol appears as the universal human destination regardless of moral standing:

- Psalm 89:48 "What man can live and never see death? Who can deliver his soul from the power of the underworld?"
- Ecclesiastes 9:10 "There is no work or thought or knowledge or wisdom in the underworld, to which you are going"
- Genesis 37:35 Jacob expects to go to Sheol to be with his son
- Job 3:11-19 Both the wicked and the weary rest in Sheol

This pattern presents Sheol as the common fate of all humanity, not as a place of punishment.

### Divine Access Pattern

While Sheol is portrayed as separated from God's active presence, it is never beyond his reach or knowledge:

- Psalm 139:8 "If I make my bed in the underworld, you are there!"
- Amos 9:2 "Though they dig into the underworld, from there shall my hand take them"
- Job 26:6 "The underworld is naked before God, and Destruction has no covering"
- Proverbs 15:11 "The underworld and Destruction lie open before the LORD"

This pattern shows that while Sheol represents separation from God's covenant blessings, it remains under divine sovereignty.

### Emotional/Metaphorical Usage Pattern

Sheol sometimes functions metaphorically to describe extreme distress or near-death experiences:

- Psalm 18:5 "The cords of the underworld surrounded me; the snares of death confronted me"
- Jonah 2:2 "Out of the belly of the underworld I called for help"
- Psalm 88:3 "My life draws near to the underworld"
- Psalm 116:3 "The snares of death encompassed me; the pangs of the underworld laid hold on me"

In these contexts, Sheol represents not actual death but the experience of being close to death or in extreme suffering.

### Prophetic Hope Pattern

In later prophetic literature, hope emerges that God might ultimately overcome Sheol:

- Hosea 13:14 "Shall I ransom them from the power of the underworld? Shall I redeem them from Death? O Death, where are your plagues? O underworld, where is your sting?"
- Isaiah 25:8 "He will swallow up death forever"
- Ezekiel 37:12-13 "I will open your graves and raise you from your graves, O my people"

This pattern shows the development of resurrection hope that would later become more explicit in apocalyptic literature and the New Testament.

## Cultural Context

### Ancient Near Eastern Concepts

The Hebrew concept of Sheol shares similarities with other ancient Near Eastern underworld concepts while maintaining distinctive features:

**Mesopotamian Parallels**

- Kur/Irkalla: The Sumerian/Akkadian underworld
- Described as a "land of no return"
- Dusty, dark realm ruled by divine figures
- Required crossing a river
- More elaborately structured than Sheol

**Egyptian Contrasts**

- Egyptian afterlife involved judgment and potential rewards
- Elaborate preparation for afterlife (mummification, tomb goods)
- Complex geography of the underworld (Duat)
- Hebrew Sheol lacks these elaborate features and judgments

**Canaanite Connections**

- Mot (Death) ruled the underworld
- Called "the pit" or "mire"
- Biblical Sheol shares terminology but lacks personification

Unlike these neighboring concepts, biblical Sheol:
- Is never ruled by a deity other than YHWH
- Contains no elaborate geography or regions
- Features no judges or judgment process
- Offers no differentiated afterlife based on earthly status

### Physical Imagery

For ancient Israelites, Sheol was conceptualized through familiar physical realities:

**The Grave Expanded**

- Individual graves (קֶבֶר, qever) provided the basic imagery
- Sheol expanded this to a collective realm
- Dark, dusty, silent qualities of tombs projected onto cosmic scale
- Family tomb practices influenced concepts of being "gathered to one's people"

**Geographical Features**

- Deep pits and caves suggested the entrance to the underworld
- Water sources sometimes associated with underworld access
- Desert wastelands as visual metaphors for Sheol's desolation
- Mountains (as world pillars) contrasted with depths of Sheol

**Agricultural Imagery**

- Seed buried in earth as metaphor for death and potential renewal
- Seasonal cycles providing context for death and life
- Dry, dusty conditions representing absence of life-giving water
- Harvest metaphors for death ("the reaper")

### Theological Development

The concept of Sheol undergoes subtle but significant development throughout the biblical period:

**Early Conception (Pentateuch/Historical Books)**

- Shadowy continuation of diminished existence
- Separation from covenant community
- Neither explicitly punishment nor reward
- "Being gathered to one's people" (Genesis 25:8, 35:29)

**Wisdom Literature Exploration**

- Job questions the finality of Sheol (14:13-14)
- Ecclesiastes emphasizes its inevitability (9:10)
- Proverbs warns of paths leading there (7:27, 15:24)
- Psalms begin to hope for rescue from Sheol (16:10, 49:15)

**Prophetic Transformation**

- Isaiah envisions Sheol's inhabitants responding to arrivals (14:9-11)
- Ezekiel depicts conversations among the dead (32:21-32)
- Hosea suggests God's potential victory over Sheol (13:14)
- Daniel explicitly mentions resurrection (12:2)

**Intertestamental Development**

- Growing belief in differentiated afterlife
- Emergence of resurrection hope
- Influence of Persian and Hellenistic concepts
- Preparation for New Testament developments

### Reading Note for English Readers

Modern readers might think of:
- Hell as a place of punishment
- Heaven/hell binary destinations
- Complete non-existence after death
- Abstract "spiritual" realm

Instead, picture:
- A shadowy, dust-filled cavern beneath the earth
- A communal grave where all ancestors reside
- A realm of whispers and diminished existence
- A place cut off from God's active presence and praise

The ancient Israelite conception of Sheol was neither the later Christian hell nor simple non-existence, but a shadowy continuation where the dead existed in a reduced state, awaiting an uncertain future that only gradually came into focus through progressive revelation.
