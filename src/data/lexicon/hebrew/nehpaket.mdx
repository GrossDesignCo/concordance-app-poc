# נֶהְפָּכֶת (Nehpaket, overturned)

Picture a city suddenly flipped upside down, its foundations exposed to the sky and its towers buried in earth – a complete reversal of the natural order where what was hidden becomes visible and what was visible becomes hidden.

## Root

ה-פ-כ (h-p-k) - To turn over, overturn, transform

Verb class: Niphal (passive/reflexive) participle, feminine singular form
- Base form: הָפַךְ (haphak) - to turn, overturn
- Niphal form: נֶהְפַּךְ (nehpak) - to be overturned
- Feminine participle: נֶהְפָּכֶת (nehpaket) - being overturned

Closest English mapping: "overturned" - indicating a complete inversion or reversal of state, often with catastrophic implications.

## In-Context

Genesis 19:21 "See, I have granted your request in this matter also; I will not **overturn** the town about which you have spoken."

Genesis 19:25 "Thus He **overturned** those cities and the entire plain, including all the inhabitants of the cities and the vegetation of the land."

Deuteronomy 29:22 "All its land is brimstone and salt, **overturned** and burnt, that it is not sown, nor does it sprout, nor does any grass grow on it, like the **overturning** of Sodom and Gomorrah, Admah and Zeboiim, which the LORD **overturned** in His anger and in His wrath."

Job 28:5 "As for the earth, from it comes bread, but underneath it is **overturned** as by fire."

Job 30:15 "Terrors are **overturned** upon me; they pursue my honor as the wind, and my prosperity has passed like a cloud."

Jonah 3:4 "And Jonah began to enter the city on the first day's walk. Then he cried out and said, 'Yet forty days, and Nineveh shall be **overturned**!'"

Amos 4:11 "I have **overturned** some of you, as God **overturned** Sodom and Gomorrah, and you were like a firebrand plucked from the burning; yet you have not returned to Me," says the LORD.

## Related Concepts

שָׁחַת (shachat) - to destroy/corrupt
- Emphasizes ruin through corruption
- Can refer to moral corruption
- "The earth was **corrupted** before God" (Genesis 6:11)

הָרַס (haras) - to tear down/demolish
- Focuses on deliberate dismantling
- Often used for walls and structures
- "You shall **tear down** their altars" (Exodus 34:13)

חָרַב (charav) - to lay waste/make desolate
- Emphasizes abandonment and desolation
- Often used for cities and lands
- "The city shall be **laid waste**" (Isaiah 24:12)

Unlike these words, נֶהְפָּכֶת (nehpaket) uniquely emphasizes the complete reversal of order or state, not just destruction but inversion of the natural arrangement.

## Compound Forms

### הֲפֵכַת סְדֹם (hapekhat Sedom) - "Overturning of Sodom"

This phrase becomes a standard biblical reference point for divine judgment:

- Deuteronomy 29:22 "Like the **overturning of Sodom** and Gomorrah, Admah and Zeboiim"
- Isaiah 13:19 "As when God **overturned** Sodom and Gomorrah"
- Jeremiah 49:18 "As in the **overthrow of** Sodom and Gomorrah"
- Amos 4:11 "I have **overturned** some of you, as God **overturned** Sodom and Gomorrah"

### מַהְפֵּכַת זָרִים (mahpekhat zarim) - "Overthrow by strangers"

- Isaiah 1:7 "Your land is desolate, your cities burned with fire; your land, strangers devour it in your presence, and it is desolate, as **overthrown by strangers**"

### נֶהְפַּךְ לְאֵבֶל (nehpak le'evel) - "Turned to mourning"

- Amos 8:10 "I will turn your feasts into mourning, and all your songs into lamentation"
- Lamentations 5:15 "The joy of our heart has ceased; our dance is **turned** into mourning"

## Greek/Septuagint Usage

In the Septuagint (LXX), נֶהְפָּכֶת is primarily translated using forms of:

καταστρέφω (katastrephō) - to overturn, overthrow
- Genesis 19:25 "And he **overthrew** (κατέστρεψεν) these cities"
- Jonah 3:4 "Yet three days and Nineveh shall be **overthrown** (καταστραφήσεται)"

This Greek word appears in the New Testament in:

Matthew 21:12 "And Jesus entered the temple and **overturned** (κατέστρεψεν) the tables of the money-changers" - Jesus' action deliberately echoes the language of divine judgment

Acts 15:16 "After this I will return, and I will rebuild the tent of David that has fallen; I will rebuild its ruins, and I will **restore** it" - Using the concept of overturning in reverse, to describe restoration

2 Peter 2:6 "If he condemned the cities of Sodom and Gomorrah to destruction by reducing them to ashes, having made them an example to those who would live ungodly lives thereafter" - While not using καταστρέφω directly, this passage references the Sodom overthrow as paradigmatic judgment

ἀναστρέφω (anastrephō) - to turn upside down, overturn
- Job 28:5 "As for the earth... underneath it is **turned up** (ἐστράφη) as by fire"

μεταστρέφω (metastrephō) - to transform, change completely
- Amos 8:10 "I will **turn** (μεταστρέψω) your feasts into mourning"

## Patterns

### Divine Judgment Pattern

The most consistent pattern with נֶהְפָּכֶת is its association with catastrophic divine judgment, particularly through the paradigmatic example of Sodom and Gomorrah:

**Physical Picture**
- Complete inversion of natural order
- Foundations exposed, structures buried
- Fire from heaven instead of rain
- Fertility replaced with sterility

**Key Passages**
- Genesis 19:25 "Thus He **overturned** those cities and the entire plain"
- Deuteronomy 29:22 "Like the **overturning** of Sodom and Gomorrah"
- Isaiah 13:19 "As when God **overturned** Sodom and Gomorrah"
- Jeremiah 50:40 "As God **overthrew** Sodom and Gomorrah and their neighboring towns"
- Lamentations 4:6 "Greater than the punishment of the sin of Sodom, that was **overthrown** as in a moment"
- Amos 4:11 "I have **overturned** some of you, as God **overturned** Sodom and Gomorrah"

This pattern establishes the "overturning" of Sodom as the archetypal example of divine judgment, referenced throughout Scripture as the standard by which other judgments are measured.

### Transformation Pattern

A secondary pattern shows נֶהְפָּכֶת used for dramatic transformations, often from positive to negative states:

**Physical Picture**
- Joy becoming sorrow
- Blessing becoming curse
- Order becoming chaos

**Key Passages**
- Amos 8:10 "I will **turn** your feasts into mourning"
- Lamentations 5:15 "Our dance is **turned** into mourning"
- Job 30:15 "Terrors are **overturned** upon me"
- Hosea 7:8 "Ephraim is a cake not **turned**" (using the same root to describe incomplete transformation)

This pattern reveals that the concept of "overturning" extends beyond physical destruction to include moral, emotional, and spiritual transformations.

### Prophetic Warning Pattern

The word appears in key prophetic warnings, serving as a final call to repentance:

**Physical Picture**
- Impending but not yet realized judgment
- Possibility of averting disaster
- Divine patience before action

**Key Passages**
- Jonah 3:4-10 "Yet forty days, and Nineveh shall be **overturned**!" But Nineveh repented, and "God relented from the disaster"
- Amos 4:11 "I have **overturned** some of you... yet you have not returned to Me"

This pattern shows that divine "overturning" is often preceded by warning and opportunity for repentance, revealing both God's justice and mercy.

## Cultural Context

### Archaeological Awareness

For ancient Israelites, the concept of נֶהְפָּכֶת would have been vividly reinforced by visible evidence in their region:

The Dead Sea area, traditionally identified with Sodom and Gomorrah, presents a stark geological contrast to the fertile Jordan Valley. Its barren landscape, salt formations, sulfur deposits, and bitumen pits would have provided a constant visual reminder of the "overturning" described in Genesis. Archaeological evidence suggests that some settlements in this region were indeed abandoned suddenly in the Early Bronze Age.

When biblical authors referenced the "overturning" of Sodom, readers would have mentally pictured not just a story but a real location they could visit—a place where life had been replaced by death, fertility by barrenness, and human habitation by desolation.

### Agricultural Significance

In an agricultural society, the concept of "overturning" carried particular weight:

- Plowing (הפך) was a positive form of "overturning" the soil to prepare for planting
- The destruction of crops was the "overturning" of agricultural blessing
- Fertile land becoming barren (as in Sodom) represented the ultimate agricultural catastrophe

This agricultural background helps explain why the "overturning" of Sodom became such a powerful metaphor—it represented not just destruction but the reversal of creation's fertility and order.

### Theological Implications

The concept of נֶהְפָּכֶת reveals important aspects of Hebrew theological understanding:

1. **Divine Sovereignty** - Only God has the power to completely "overturn" the created order
2. **Moral Order** - "Overturning" is not random but connected to moral violations
3. **Covenant Warning** - The "overturning" of Sodom serves as a warning to Israel about covenant faithfulness
4. **Mercy Within Judgment** - As seen in Jonah, even pronounced "overturning" can be averted through repentance

### Reading Note for English Readers

Modern readers might think of:
- Simple destruction or demolition
- Flipping something over
- Political revolution or coup
- Overturning a legal decision

Instead, picture:
- A city not just destroyed but inverted
- The natural order completely reversed
- Divine intervention that exposes hidden corruption
- A transformation so complete that the original state becomes unrecognizable

The comprehensive nature of נֶהְפָּכֶת reminds us that divine judgment isn't merely punitive but represents the exposure and reversal of a moral order that has already been corrupted from within.
