# כָּתְנוֹת (Kotnot, tunics)

Picture a simple garment that covers the body from shoulders to knees or ankles - not merely clothing, but the first divinely-crafted covering that transformed nakedness into dignity, marking both human vulnerability and divine provision.

## Root

כ-ת-נ (k-t-n) - To cover the body with a close-fitting garment

Closest English mapping: "tunics" - indicating a simple, shirt-like garment that covers the torso and typically extends below the waist.

Noun form, feminine plural (כָּתְנוֹת, kotnot)
Singular form: כְּתֹנֶת (ketonet)

## In-Context

Genesis 3:21 "And the LORD God made for Adam and for his wife **tunics** of skin and clothed them."
- First divine craftsmanship
- Replacement for inadequate fig leaves
- Made from animal skins, implying sacrifice

Genesis 37:3 "Now Israel loved Joseph more than any other of his sons, because he was the son of his old age. And he made him a **tunic** of many colors."
- Symbol of paternal favor
- Distinctive in appearance
- Cause of fraternal jealousy

Genesis 37:31-33 "Then they took Joseph's **tunic** and slaughtered a goat and dipped the **tunic** in the blood. And they sent the **tunic** of many colors and brought it to their father and said, 'This we have found; please identify whether it is your son's **tunic** or not.' And he identified it and said, 'It is my son's **tunic**. A fierce animal has devoured him.'"
- Used deceptively
- Blood-stained as false evidence
- Instrument of grief

Exodus 28:4 "These are the garments that they shall make: a breastpiece, an ephod, a robe, a coat of checker work, a turban, and a sash. They shall make holy garments for Aaron your brother and his sons to serve me as priests."
- Here כְּתֹנֶת is translated as "coat" in many English versions
- Part of priestly garments
- Holy purpose

Exodus 28:39-40 "You shall weave the **tunic** in checker work of fine linen, and you shall make a turban of fine linen, and you shall make a sash embroidered with needlework. For Aaron's sons you shall make **tunics** and sashes and caps."
- Made of fine linen
- Woven in checkered pattern for high priest
- Part of sacred vestments

Leviticus 8:7 "And he put the **tunic** on him and tied the sash around his waist and clothed him with the robe and put the ephod on him and tied the skillfully woven band of the ephod around him, binding it to him with the band."
- Part of priestly investiture ceremony
- Put on in specific order
- Signifies official role

Leviticus 10:5 "So they went near and carried them in their **tunics** out of the camp, as Moses had said."
- Reference to Nadab and Abihu
- Tunics became burial garments
- Carried out in their priestly garments after judgment

2 Samuel 13:18-19 "Now she was wearing a long **tunic** with sleeves, for thus were the virgin daughters of the king dressed. So his servant put her out and bolted the door after her. And Tamar put ashes on her head and tore the long **tunic** that she wore."
- Sign of royal virgin status
- Torn as sign of grief/violation
- Specifically described as having sleeves

## Related Concepts

שִׂמְלָה (simlah) - Outer garment/cloak
- Larger, looser covering worn over the tunic
- Often used as covering at night
- "If you take your neighbor's **cloak** in pledge..." (Exodus 22:26)

בֶּגֶד (beged) - Garment/clothing (general term)
- Generic term for any clothing
- Broader category that includes tunics
- "They shall make holy **garments** for Aaron" (Exodus 28:4)

אֵפוֹד (ephod) - Priestly vestment
- Worn over the tunic by priests
- More ceremonial than functional
- "He put the **ephod** on him" (Leviticus 8:7)

מְעִיל (me'il) - Robe/mantle
- Outer ceremonial garment
- Often associated with status
- "Samuel was ministering... dressed in a linen **ephod**" (1 Samuel 2:18)

Unlike these words, כָּתְנוֹת (kotnot) specifically refers to the basic body-covering garment worn directly against the skin, forming the foundation of one's clothing.

## Greek/Septuagint Usage

In the Septuagint (LXX), כָּתְנוֹת is primarily translated as:

χιτών (chitōn) - tunic/garment
- Basic meaning: inner garment worn next to skin
- Genesis 3:21: "καὶ ἐποίησεν κύριος ὁ θεὸς τῷ Αδαμ καὶ τῇ γυναικὶ αὐτοῦ **χιτῶνας** δερματίνους καὶ ἐνέδυσεν αὐτούς"
- "And the Lord God made for Adam and his wife **tunics** of skin and clothed them"

This same word χιτών appears in the New Testament:

Matthew 5:40 "And if anyone would sue you and take your tunic, let him have your cloak as well."
- Jesus contrasts χιτών (inner garment) with ἱμάτιον (outer garment)
- Shows the tunic as the basic, essential garment

Matthew 10:10 "No bag for your journey, or two tunics or sandals or a staff..."
- Basic necessity for travelers
- Disciples instructed to travel with minimal possessions

John 19:23 "When the soldiers had crucified Jesus, they took his garments and divided them into four parts, one part for each soldier; also his tunic. But the tunic was seamless, woven in one piece from top to bottom."
- Special seamless χιτών of Jesus
- Soldiers cast lots rather than tear it
- Possible allusion to high priest's garment

Acts 9:39 "...and all the widows stood beside him weeping and showing tunics and other garments that Dorcas made while she was with them."
- Practical clothing made for the poor
- Sign of charity and service

## Patterns

### Divine Covering Pattern

The כְּתֹנֶת first appears as God's solution to human vulnerability and shame, establishing a pattern of divine covering that runs throughout Scripture:

Genesis 3:21 "And the LORD God made for Adam and for his wife **tunics** of skin and clothed them."
- First divine craftsmanship after the Fall
- Replacement for human-made coverings (fig leaves)
- Required sacrifice (animal skins)
- God personally clothed them

This pattern continues through:

Exodus 28:39-40 "You shall weave the **tunic** in checker work of fine linen..."
- Divine design for priestly coverings
- Specific instructions from God
- Covering that enables approaching God

Isaiah 61:10 "I will greatly rejoice in the LORD... for he has clothed me with the garments of salvation; he has covered me with the robe of righteousness"
- Metaphorical extension of divine covering
- Salvation depicted as God-provided garment
- Covering shame with righteousness

Zechariah 3:3-5 "Now Joshua was standing before the angel, clothed with filthy garments... 'Remove the filthy garments from him.' And to him he said, 'Behold, I have taken your iniquity away from you, and I will clothe you with pure vestments.'"
- Priestly garments as symbol of spiritual state
- Divine replacement of unclean with clean
- Restoration through divine covering

Revelation 7:9,13-14 "...clothed in white robes... These are the ones coming out of the great tribulation. They have washed their robes and made them white in the blood of the Lamb."
- Ultimate fulfillment of divine covering
- Garments washed in sacrificial blood
- Covering that enables standing before God's throne

This pattern reveals that from Eden to eternity, God provides covering for human vulnerability and shame through sacrifice.

### Identity Marker Pattern

Throughout Scripture, the כְּתֹנֶת functions as a visible sign of identity and status:

Genesis 37:3 "Now Israel loved Joseph more than any other of his sons... And he made him a **tunic** of many colors."
- Marked Joseph as favored son
- Visible sign of special status
- Recognized even when bloodstained

Exodus 28:4,40 "These are the garments that they shall make... For Aaron's sons you shall make **tunics** and sashes and caps."
- Distinguished priests from ordinary Israelites
- Marked them as set apart for divine service
- Required for tabernacle/temple ministry

2 Samuel 13:18 "Now she was wearing a long **tunic** with sleeves, for thus were the virgin daughters of the king dressed."
- Identified royal virgin daughters
- Public sign of protected status
- Tearing signified violation of that status

Ezra 2:69 "...and 100 priests' **garments**" (כָּתְנוֹת)
- Counted among temple treasures
- Essential for resuming proper worship
- Symbol of restored priestly service

This pattern shows how clothing in biblical narrative goes beyond mere covering to function as a visible embodiment of one's role, status, and relationship to both God and community.

### Deception and Truth Pattern

The כְּתֹנֶת appears prominently in narratives involving deception and revelation:

Genesis 37:31-33 "Then they took Joseph's **tunic** and slaughtered a goat and dipped the **tunic** in the blood... And he identified it and said, 'It is my son's **tunic**. A fierce animal has devoured him.'"
- Used to deceive Jacob
- Blood-stained as false evidence
- Instrument of grief and deception

Genesis 37:23 "So when Joseph came to his brothers, they stripped him of his **tunic**, the **tunic** of many colors that he wore."
- Stripping represented rejection of identity
- Removal of father's favor
- Precursor to enslavement

Leviticus 10:5 "So they went near and carried them in their **tunics** out of the camp..."
- Nadab and Abihu's priestly garments became burial shrouds
- Contrast between appearance and reality
- Garments that should have signified honor became signs of judgment

2 Samuel 13:18-19 "And Tamar put ashes on her head and tore the long **tunic** that she wore."
- Deliberate destruction of identity marker
- Public declaration of violation
- Truth-telling through garment alteration

This pattern reveals how garments in biblical narrative often function as powerful symbols in the tension between appearance and reality, deception and truth.

## Cultural Context

### Ancient Near Eastern Garments

The כְּתֹנֶת was the most basic and universal garment in the ancient Near East, worn by people of all social classes:

For ancient Israelites, the basic tunic was typically made of wool or linen, reaching to the knees or ankles. While the poor might own only a single tunic, the wealthy would possess multiple garments of finer materials. Egyptian tomb paintings and Mesopotamian reliefs show similar basic garments across cultures, though with regional variations.

The simplest tunics were made from rectangular pieces of cloth sewn together with openings for head and arms. More elaborate versions, like Joseph's and the royal daughters', featured long sleeves and decorative elements. Archaeological findings confirm that colored and patterned textiles were luxury items, explaining why Joseph's special tunic provoked such jealousy.

In Egyptian contexts, fine linen tunics were associated with purity and status, paralleling the biblical emphasis on linen for priestly garments. Mesopotamian texts mention special garments for religious functionaries that were believed to carry divine presence, similar to how the biblical priestly tunics were consecrated.

### Theological Significance

The כְּתֹנֶת carries profound theological significance in biblical narrative:

1. **Divine Provision**: The first tunics in Genesis 3 represent God's gracious provision despite human sin. Unlike the inadequate fig leaves, God provides a covering that truly addresses shame and vulnerability. This establishes a pattern where God, not human effort, provides adequate covering for sin.

2. **Sacrifice and Covering**: The animal skins used for the first tunics implicitly introduce the concept of sacrifice for covering, foreshadowing the sacrificial system and ultimately Christ's sacrifice. The Hebrew mind would connect these first garments with the later priestly garments, seeing a continuity in how God covers human shame.

3. **Embodied Identity**: In Hebrew thought, garments were not merely external coverings but extensions of the person. When Jacob recognized Joseph's bloodied tunic, he declared "my son is torn to pieces" - the garment represented the person. This explains why priestly garments were considered holy and why changing garments often signified spiritual transformation.

4. **Honor and Shame**: The cultural dynamics of honor and shame are central to understanding the כְּתֹנֶת. Adam and Eve's nakedness represented shame; God's tunics restored honor. Joseph's special tunic conferred honor; its stripping imposed shame. Tamar's royal tunic signified protected status; its tearing publicly declared her shameful treatment. Throughout, garments function as visible markers in the honor-shame system.

### Reading Note for English Readers

Modern readers might think of:
- Simple t-shirts or undergarments
- Fashion choices with primarily aesthetic value
- Disposable items easily replaced

Instead, picture:
- The essential garment that transforms nakedness into dignity
- A visible extension of one's identity and status
- An item so valuable it might be one's primary possession
- A covering that represents divine provision and protection

In a culture where most owned only one or two garments, and where clothing was laboriously handmade, the כְּתֹנֶת represented not just covering but care, identity, and divine concern for human vulnerability.
